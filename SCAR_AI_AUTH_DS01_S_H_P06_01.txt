Personally, I only have experience with traffic in two cities, London where I grew up and work, and St Albans where I currently live. Taking London as an example, it is the capital city and the sheer number of people who still drive to work despite the rising costs of the congestion charge and low emission zones means the capacity of the roads simply cannot handle that volume in such a condensed period. Bus lanes remove one of the two available lanes from general use, so traffic is forced into a single lane rather than two, which is fine for public transport but clearly more people are still using their own vehicles. There is also the issue that there is often only one person per car. At the same time, you have children going to school in the same time slot as commuters going to work, adding another large group to the congestion. Traffic lights not working or inefficient traffic management systems create bottlenecks at regular intervals, and roadworks drastically slow movement, particularly during rush hour. You also have people commuting into the city from outside because higher paid jobs are concentrated there, meaning more people are travelling inward simultaneously than outward, which reverses in the evening and creates a persistent imbalance in traffic flow in both directions.
