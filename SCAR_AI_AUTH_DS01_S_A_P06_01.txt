Traffic congestion in a city is primarily driven by an imbalance between road capacity and the volume of vehicles using it, often worsened by poor planning and behavioural patterns. Rapid urbanisation increases population density without a proportional expansion of infrastructure, leaving roads unable to cope with demand. A heavy reliance on private cars rather than efficient public transport intensifies this pressure, particularly during peak commuting hours when large numbers of people travel simultaneously. Inefficient traffic management systems, such as poorly timed signals or inadequate junction design, can further slow movement and create bottlenecks that ripple across the network. Roadworks, accidents, and unpredictable events disrupt normal flow and reduce available lanes, amplifying delays. In many cities, limited parking availability leads to vehicles circulating in search of spaces, adding unnecessary traffic. Additionally, economic activity concentrates in central areas, drawing large volumes of vehicles into confined spaces not designed for such intensity. Human behaviour also plays a role, with inconsistent driving, hesitation, and minor errors collectively reducing overall efficiency. Without coordinated urban planning, investment in public transport, and smarter traffic control, these factors compound, making congestion a persistent and often worsening problem.
