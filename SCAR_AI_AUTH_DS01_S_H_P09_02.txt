There are multiple reasons why recycling systems tend to fail, starting from the end user where confusion exists about what is recyclable and which bin rubbish should go in. Multi material packaging is difficult to separate, and food waste contaminating recyclable rubbish is a persistent problem. Moving from household to collection level, even if one person recycles correctly, if others on the same road do not, the whole lorry load can become contaminated and unusable. It is a very difficult process to get right consistently. The recycling process itself is also geared more towards profit than environmental benefit. Processes are slow and outdated, struggling to keep up with demand and creating bottlenecks that make the system inefficient. Even when everything is done correctly, there is still the question of, does anyone want to buy the recycled material. If not, it must be stored at cost or ends up in landfill or incinerated, which defeats the purpose. Making new plastic is generally cheaper than recycling old plastic, and recycled material often produces inferior secondary materials. Until the dynamics change and processes modernise, recycling will continue to fail.
