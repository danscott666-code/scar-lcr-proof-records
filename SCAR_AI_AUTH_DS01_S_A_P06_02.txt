Traffic congestion in a city is primarily driven by a mismatch between road capacity and travel demand, amplified by structural and behavioural factors. Rapid urbanisation concentrates large populations into limited space, increasing the volume of daily trips for work, education, and services. When private car use dominates due to inadequate or unreliable public transport, the road network becomes saturated, particularly during peak hours. Infrastructure constraints such as narrow roads, poor junction design, and insufficient parking exacerbate bottlenecks, while roadworks and poorly coordinated traffic signals further disrupt flow. Economic activity also plays a role, as delivery vehicles, ride-hailing services, and commercial transport add to traffic density. Human behaviour contributes significantly, with inefficient driving patterns, frequent lane changes, and hesitation at junctions reducing throughput. Incidents such as accidents or vehicle breakdowns can trigger cascading delays across the network. Additionally, urban planning decisions, including zoning that separates residential and commercial areas, force longer commutes and concentrate traffic along key corridors. Weather conditions and seasonal fluctuations can also degrade road performance. Overall, congestion emerges not from a single cause but from the interaction of infrastructure limits, policy choices, and collective travel behaviour.
