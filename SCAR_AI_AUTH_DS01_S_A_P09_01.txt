Recycling systems often fail in practice because they depend on a chain of human behaviour, economic viability, and technical capability that rarely aligns cleanly. At the front end, households are expected to sort waste correctly, but contamination is widespread; even small amounts of food residue or incorrect materials can render entire batches unusable. This problem is compounded by inconsistent rules between local authorities, which confuses users and reduces compliance. Further down the chain, the economics are fragile. Recycled materials must compete with cheaper virgin resources, especially when oil prices are low, making plastics recycling financially unattractive. As a result, collected recyclables are sometimes exported or even diverted to landfill or incineration despite being sorted. Technically, many products are not designed for recycling in the first place, using mixed materials, dyes, or adhesives that are difficult or impossible to separate efficiently. Packaging labelled as recyclable often only applies under ideal industrial conditions that are not widely available. Finally, there is a structural mismatch between what is collected and what markets actually demand, leading to stockpiling or disposal. In reality, recycling systems are expected to compensate for poor product design and consumption habits, which makes consistent success unlikely without upstream changes.
