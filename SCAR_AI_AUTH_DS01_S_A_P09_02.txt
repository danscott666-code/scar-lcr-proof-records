Recycling systems often fail in practice because they rely on a chain of assumptions that break down under real-world conditions. At the household level, people frequently misunderstand what can and cannot be recycled, leading to contamination of waste streams with food residue, mixed materials, or non-recyclable items. Even small levels of contamination can render entire batches unusable, making them more expensive to process than to discard. On the infrastructure side, sorting facilities are limited by technology and cost constraints, meaning complex packaging such as multi-layer plastics cannot be efficiently separated and recovered. Economic factors are equally decisive; when the market price for recycled materials drops below the cost of collection and processing, there is little incentive for companies to prioritise recycling over landfill or incineration. Additionally, many products are not designed with recyclability in mind, forcing systems to handle materials that were never intended to be recovered. Policy inconsistency and weak regulation further undermine effectiveness, as standards vary by region and enforcement is often minimal. The result is a system that appears functional in theory but struggles operationally, where environmental intent is outweighed by logistical complexity and economic reality.
